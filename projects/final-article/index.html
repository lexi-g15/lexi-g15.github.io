As the abortion rate drops, some states are making it more difficult for women to access clinics

Alexandra Gopin | December 10, 2020
Photo taken from Creative Commons, link.

Abortion and reproductive rights are topics that have been debated in this country for decades. The debate picked up during the 1960s and finally the Supreme Court decision on Roe v. Wade in 1973 made abortion legal. However, this law allowed for states to decide their own implications and policies within the wider national protection of abortion. With some states remaining strictly against abortion, this has allowed them to work around the law and bar women from accessing abortions. The debate over abortion rights still continues today.

Abortion rates decreased in this country significantly from 2014 to 2017, according to data compiled by the Guttmacher institute. Rates have since increased although they are still relatively low, according to The World Population Review. The cause for drop in abortion is hard to define. However, it likely depends on the state a person is in, which policies are in place and how many abortion clinics are available to women.

Data on abortion show that the rate of abortion in the United States dropped by eight percent from 2014 to 2017. In 2017, there were only 13.5 abortions per every 1,000 women. Abortion rates overall for this year are up to 11 percent. National pregnancy rates also declined overall, one of the top explanations for the decline in abortion rate. However, the rates also vary by state.

In 2017, there were 1,587 facilities available nationwide. However, the Abortion Care Network reported that there were 26 closures of independent abortion care providers nationwide as of November 2019. The amount of clinics per state also varies.

The national average of unintended pregnancies of women who recently gave birth and did not want to become pregnant remained at 30.3% in 2019, according to a study by the United Health Foundation. Meaning there are still women experiencing unintended pregnancies even though states are restricting access to abortions through policy changes to date.

The national debate over abortion was most recently rekindled when President Trump nominated Amy Coney Barrett, who does not believe in pro-choice policies, to the Supreme Court.

Two states, Alabama and Maryland, represent highly different views and policies on abortion and reproductive rigths.

In Alambama, data show that abortion rates declined by 23 percent from 8.3 to 6.4 abortions per 1,000 women from 2014 to 2017. This year, Alabama has reported an abortion rate of about six percent so far, according to the World Population Reveiw.

However, the rate of unintended pregnancies in Alabama, according to the same United Health Foundation study, is 40 percent, about 10 percent more than the national average.

Despite this, Alabama passed the strictest abortion policy in the nation last year. The law, called the The Alabama Human Life Protection Act, bans abortion unless a pregnancy presents a serious health risk for the woman. The law also made the act of providing an abortion a felony.

Meanwhile, between 2014 and 2019, an independent abortion clinic closed, leaving just six clinics open in the state. In 2017, 93 percent of Alabama counties had no clinics even though 59 percent of women lived in those counties.

In contrast, Maryland has few governmental restrictions on abortion and many more clinics available.

Data from Maryland show that there was a seven percent increase in abortion from 2014 to 2017 with 23.4 to 25 abortions per 1,000 women. This year, Maryland has an abortion rate of 25 percent.

The rate of unintended pregnancies, however, is 31.5 percent, which is much closer to the national average unlike Alabama.

Maryland enacted the Freedom of Choice Act in 1991, which ensures a woman’s right to abortion even if Roe v. Wade is overturned in the Supreme Court.

In 2017 there were also 44 clinics available to women in Maryland and only six closures of independent clinics between 2014 and 2019 according to the Abortion Care Network. Although the Guttmacher Institute reported that 71 percent of Maryland counties did not have clinics in 2017, only 29 percent of women lived in these counties. Meaning the majority of the population had access to clinics.

Although some studies concluded that the dropping abortion rate might be due to lower pregnancy rates and better use of contraceptives, the laws restricting abortion in different areas can’t be ignored. There are higher rates of unintended pregnancy and lower abortion rates along with less access to clinics in areas with stricter abortion laws, like Alabama. There are also lower rates of unintended pregnancies and higher abortion rates along with more access to clinics in areas with pro-choice laws, like Maryland. It can therefore be concluded that there are women unintentionally getting pregnant without proper access to abortion due to laws, restrictions and clinic closures in stricter and more conservative areas of the nation.
